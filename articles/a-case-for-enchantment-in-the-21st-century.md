# A Case for Enchantment in the 21st Century

> The astronomer measuring the death of stars has not made the heavens poorer.
<!-- more -->

Listen—

you are not wrong
to miss the old enchantments.

You are not childish
for wanting the forest
to be more than wood,
or the night
to be more than absence,
or the stars
to be more than fire
burning without intention
through the cold arithmetic
of space.

There was once, or seemed to be,
a nearness in things.

The old people knew it.

They sat by the campfire
with smoke in their clothes
and sparks moving upward
like brief souls
returning to some forgotten country,

and when they spoke
of spirits in the trees,
or a presence by the river,
or a voice heard once
beyond the last field at dusk,

they were not always trying
to tell you what was factual.

They were trying
to tell you
that the world was alive.

That the earth was not mute.
That darkness was not empty.
That the night
had a face turned toward us.

And you feel the loss of that.
Of course you do.

Because you were born
into the afterworld of explanation,

into the long afternoon
after the gods had fallen silent,

after thunder became weather,
after the ghost in the hallway
became grief and bad sleep,
after the demon in the blood
became chemistry,

and the madman in the marketplace
announced that God was dead,

though perhaps
what died first
was not God,

but the old sense
that the universe
might lean close
and answer you.

And yet—

do not think
the world has been emptied.

Do not think
the naming of a thing
has taken away its power.

The biologist bending over moss
has not diminished the forest.

The astronomer
measuring the death of stars
has not made the heavens poorer.

The doctor
pulling fever from the body
has not stolen mystery
from suffering.

No—

they have only led you
through one door
and left you standing
before a greater one.

For what is more magical:

a spirit in the tree,

or that the tree
should rise at all

out of blind soil,
drinking light,
making from air and silence
its green, patient body?

What is more astonishing:

a god hurling stars
across the sky,

or that stars should exist,

that they should burn
for ages beyond thought,

that they should die
and scatter the metals
that become your blood,

so that you,
brief and desiring,
can stand on a small world
under the night
and ache for meaning?

You grieve
not because mystery is gone,

but because it moved.

Once it sat
near the village.

Once it lived
just beyond the firelight.

Once it breathed
from the forest edge
and let itself be imagined
as ghost, god, omen, beast.

Now it has gone deeper.

It lives in the atom.
In time.
In consciousness.
In gravity.
In the birth of galaxies.
In the first spark of life
lifting itself from the dark waters
and beginning the long liturgy
of evolution.

It lives
in the strange tenderness
of your own mind,

that pile of star-forged matter
which has somehow become able
to love beauty,

to remember the dead,
to fear the silence,
to ask if anything hears us.

And perhaps something does.

Not as the old stories said.
Not as a face in the fire.
Not as footsteps
at the edge of the clearing.

But in another way.

Perhaps the universe
does speak back,

only not in the old human tongue.

Perhaps it answers
by giving you this blue planet,
this turning world of oceans and leaves.

Perhaps it answers
through the bright wound of a star,
through the black flowering of space,

through the fact
that out of all this vastness
something has appeared
that can kneel beside a campfire
and call the whole thing beautiful.

Perhaps that is the speech:

not command,
not miracle,
not apparition,

but invitation.

To look longer.
To listen differently.
To let knowledge
make you more reverent,
not less.

To understand
that the death of one kind of magic
was not the death of magic itself,

only the falling away
of a nearer language.

And yes,
there is sorrow in that.

There is sorrow
in no longer believing
the trees keep spirits
in their bark,

or that the dead
wait in the rafters,

or that the sky
is a great listening kingdom
arranged for our sake.

There is sorrow
in becoming modern.

In being orphaned
from the old intimacy.

In inheriting a cosmos
more immense than any myth
and less personal
than a prayer whispered
into the wind.

But listen to me—

the world is still enchanted.

Not because it breaks its laws,
but because it has laws
at all.

Not because ghosts
move through walls,

but because there are walls,
and light upon them,

and minds that can remember
who once touched them.

Not because a god
waits in the storm,

but because the storm itself
is so fierce, so ordered,
so ancient,

so beautiful in its indifference,

that it leaves you trembling
as if someone had spoken.

And perhaps someone has.

Perhaps the old tales
were not false,

only unfinished.

Perhaps the campfires
were early observatories.

Perhaps the elders,
with their weathered hands
and smoke-dark voices,

were trying to tell you
what science later told you
in another dialect:

that you belong
to a world far stranger
than your habits allow;

that the universe
is not dead matter,

but a vast, ongoing astonishment;

that you are not separate
from the mystery,

but one of its brief
and luminous expressions.

So when you feel that grief,

that longing
for ghosts in the hallway,

for gods in the thunder,

for spirits in the pines,

for the night
to feel inhabited—

do not be ashamed.

It is the soul remembering
its ancient appetite.

It is the heart refusing
to live in a flattened world.

It is the child in you
still turning toward the dark,

not because he wishes
to be deceived,

but because he senses
that reality,

even now,

has depths
we have only begun to name.

And if, one evening,
you find yourself again
by a fire,

with old voices around you

and the trees standing beyond
like dark witnesses,

and the stars above
opening one by one
in the great black fabric,

do not ask
whether the world is magical.

Ask instead
whether you are quiet enough
to hear it.

Because it is speaking.

It has always been speaking.

In leaf and flame,

in grief and love,

in the silence between galaxies,

in the long memory of stone,

in the dark rivers of the blood,

in the old stories,

in the new equations,

in the ashes rising,

in the first star of evening
appearing like a wound
that has chosen to shine.

And it is speaking now
to the part of you
that still hopes
the universe
might look back.
